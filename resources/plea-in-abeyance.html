What Is a Plea in Abeyance?

One of the most useful — and most misunderstood — tools in Utah criminal law. Here's how it actually works, in plain English.

If you're facing a first-time misdemeanor in Utah, there's a good chance someone has mentioned the phrase "plea in abeyance" to you — and an equally good chance nobody explained what it really means. It's one of the most valuable options available to many defendants, but because it starts with entering a guilty plea, it's also easy to misunderstand and easy to get wrong. This guide walks through what it is, how it plays out step by step, and the trade-offs worth understanding before you agree to anything.

The short version

A plea in abeyance is governed by Utah Code Title 77, Chapter 2a. In plain terms: you enter a plea of guilty or no contest, but the court does not, at that moment, enter a conviction against you or sentence you. Instead, the plea is held "in abeyance" — set aside — while you complete a set of conditions over an agreed period of time. Complete them, and the charge can be dismissed or reduced. Fail to complete them, and the plea you already entered is used to convict and sentence you. That two-sided nature is the whole point: it offers a real path to walking away without a conviction, in exchange for real accountability up front.

The key trade-off in one sentence

You're trading the certainty of a guilty plea now for the opportunity to have the charge dismissed later — which only pays off if you complete every condition of the agreement.

How it works, step by step

1. Negotiation. A plea in abeyance is not something you're automatically entitled to. It has to be negotiated with the prosecutor, who must agree to it — and the court must accept it. This is where having counsel matters most, because the terms are negotiable and the first offer isn't always the best one available.

2. Entering the plea. You formally enter a plea of guilty or no contest, in full compliance with the rules of criminal procedure, including a knowing waiver of certain rights. The court accepts the plea but withholds judgment.

3. The written agreement. The specific terms are set out in a written plea in abeyance agreement signed by you, your attorney, and the prosecutor. This document controls everything that follows — what you must do, how long you have, and what happens at the end.

4. The compliance period. You complete the agreed conditions over the set time frame. By statute, this period generally may not exceed 18 months for a misdemeanor, or three years for a felony or combination of misdemeanors, though many agreements run closer to 12 months.

5. Completion. If you satisfy every condition, the court — as provided in the agreement — may allow you to withdraw your plea and dismiss the case, or reduce the offense to a lower degree and enter judgment on that lesser offense.

6. Or non-completion. If you don't meet the conditions, the prosecutor can move to end the abeyance. After a hearing, the court enters judgment on the plea you already gave and proceeds to sentencing. You've effectively used up the plea, so this is the outcome the whole process is designed to help you avoid.

Common conditions

Conditions are tailored to the case, but they commonly include: committing no new criminal offenses during the abeyance period; paying a plea in abeyance fee (plus any restitution owed to a victim); and completing whatever remedial or rehabilitative program fits the charge — a substance abuse class for a drug case, an anger management or similar course for an assault case, traffic school for certain driving offenses. In general, a court can attach any condition it could have imposed as a term of probation for the same offense.

A real-world example

Consider a first-time class B misdemeanor drug possession charge with no aggravating facts. A common resolution: the defendant enters a plea in abeyance with a 12-month period, agrees to commit no new offenses, pays the fee, and completes a substance abuse assessment and any recommended class. Twelve months later, having done everything required, the defendant returns to court, the plea is withdrawn, and the case is dismissed — leaving no conviction on the record. Had the same person simply pleaded guilty at the first hearing, they'd carry that conviction permanently unless and until it could later be expunged. Same starting charge, very different long-term result.

Which charges are excluded

Not everything qualifies. The most important exclusion to know: DUI cannot be held in abeyance — Utah law specifically prohibits it. Certain other offenses are restricted as well, and because both the prosecutor and the court must consent, prosecutors often decline based on the severity of the charge, the facts involved, or a defendant's prior history. Eligibility is genuinely case-by-case, which is why it's worth an honest evaluation rather than an assumption in either direction.

The trade-offs to understand before signing

A plea in abeyance is a strong tool, but it isn't free of risk. You are pleading guilty — and if you don't complete the conditions, that plea is what convicts you, with your trial rights already waived. During the abeyance period itself, the charge can still appear on some background checks until the case is dismissed and expunged. And the conditions are real obligations with real deadlines. For the right case and the right person, the upside clearly outweighs those risks. But it's a decision worth making with clear eyes and, ideally, with counsel who can tell you honestly whether it's the best available path for your situation.

Frequently asked

Plea in abeyance questions.

What is a plea in abeyance in Utah?

Under Utah Code Title 77, Chapter 2a, a plea in abeyance is an agreement where you enter a guilty or no contest plea, but the court holds off on entering a conviction or imposing a sentence while you complete a set of agreed conditions over a period of time. If you complete them, the charge can be dismissed or reduced.

Does a plea in abeyance mean the charge is dismissed?

It can be, if you complete every condition of the agreement. Depending on what the agreement provides, the court may allow you to withdraw your plea and dismiss the case, or reduce the offense to a lower degree. If you don't complete the conditions, the plea already entered is used to convict and sentence you.

What charges are not eligible for a plea in abeyance in Utah?

DUI is excluded by statute — a DUI plea cannot be held in abeyance. Certain other offenses are also restricted, and both the prosecutor and the court must agree before any plea can be held in abeyance, so eligibility depends heavily on the specific charge and facts.

How long does a plea in abeyance last in Utah?

The period is set in the agreement. For misdemeanors it generally may not exceed 18 months, and for a felony or a combination of misdemeanors it generally may not exceed three years, though many agreements run around 12 months.
